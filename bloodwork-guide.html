Bloodwork 101:
Read Your Own Results

Your doctor's "normal range" is built on population averages — including sick, sedentary, and metabolically compromised people. This guide tells you what optimal looks like for a high-functioning man over 40, what to watch, and when to act.

Key Takeaways

  • Normal and optimal are not the same thing. Aim for optimal ranges, not just "not flagged."
  • Total testosterone without SHBG is an incomplete picture. Free testosterone is what matters.
  • T3 (active thyroid hormone) is rarely tested by GPs — request it specifically.
  • Ferritin and vitamin D are chronically low in active men and almost never checked proactively.
  • Bloodwork is a snapshot, not a verdict. Trend over time beats a single data point.
01

Testosterone & Sex Hormones

Testosterone is the primary anabolic hormone in men — responsible for muscle mass, fat distribution, libido, mood, cognitive drive, and bone density. Total testosterone is the most commonly tested marker, but it is an incomplete picture without SHBG.

Sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG) is a protein that binds testosterone and renders it biologically inactive. A man can have a "normal" total testosterone but significantly suppressed free testosterone if SHBG is elevated. Free testosterone — the portion available for use by tissues — is the figure that actually matters.

02

Thyroid Panel

Most GPs only test TSH — the pituitary signal telling the thyroid to produce hormones. This is like checking if the gas pedal is being pressed without checking whether the engine is responding. A full thyroid panel includes TSH, free T4, and — critically — free T3, the active hormone that drives cellular metabolism.

Many men with metabolic suppression, low waking temperature, poor body composition, fatigue, and brain fog have a TSH within range but suboptimal free T3. This is particularly common after periods of chronic calorie restriction, high stress, or poor sleep — all of which suppress T4-to-T3 conversion.

What to Ask Your Doctor

"I'd like a full thyroid panel including free T3 and reverse T3, not just TSH." Most GPs will not order this by default. You need to ask for it specifically — or use a private lab.

03

Cortisol & Stress Markers

Serum cortisol is a snapshot — it fluctuates significantly across the day and in response to acute stressors including the blood draw itself. A morning fasted cortisol (taken 7–9am) gives the most useful reading. Salivary cortisol testing across 4 time points (morning, noon, afternoon, evening) is the gold standard for identifying chronic dysregulation.

05

When to Act on Results

A single out-of-range result is rarely an emergency — context matters. Before assuming a problem, consider: when was it drawn (time of day, fasted vs. fed), what was your stress load that week, are you mid-training block or post-deload, and have you had any illness recently?

  • →Retest before concluding anything. Confirm a pattern across two draws, ideally 6–8 weeks apart under similar conditions. One suboptimal result is a data point; two is a trend worth addressing.
  • →Address lifestyle variables first. Low testosterone, suboptimal free T3, and elevated cortisol all respond significantly to sleep, nutrition, and stress interventions — before any pharmaceutical or hormonal approach is considered.
  • →Bring results to your coach before your GP. Your coach can contextualise the results within your training and nutrition picture. GPs will often act on population-range flags that are not clinically meaningful for a well-trained, well-fuelled man.
  • →Target the trend, not the snapshot. The goal is progressive improvement across quarterly testing — not perfection on a single panel.
