Training Principles

Training & Recovery
Over 40

Training in your 40s is not the same game as training in your 20s — and the men who try to run the same playbook eventually pay for it. The Reboot Method is not about training less. It is about training with precision, recovering harder, and building a body that gets stronger every year rather than grinding itself down.

Key Takeaways

  • Form and technique execution is the #1 priority in this programme — full stop, before anything else.
  • All clients must record and submit training footage regularly for coach assessment.
  • Every working set should be loaded so you fail within the programmed rep range — not before it, not comfortably past it.
  • All programmed sets are working sets. We do not use pyramid-style warm-up-into-working-set structures.
  • We train 3-5 days per week. More is not better — results come from the maximum stimulus you can actually recover from.
  • Deloads are triggered by a genuine dip in performance or energy, not by a fixed calendar.
01

Priority One: Form & Technique Execution

Before progressive overload, before rep ranges, before anything else in this programme — form and technique execution is the priority. A heavier weight lifted with compromised form is not progress. It's borrowed time against an injury that will eventually cost you far more training days than the slower, correct progression would have.

This isn't a beginner-only concern. Some of the most common form breakdowns we see are in men who've trained for years and have simply never had their technique properly assessed — compensations that have become so habitual they don't even register as a problem until something gets injured.

Mandatory: Submit Training Footage

All clients must record and submit training footage regularly for coach assessment. This is not optional and not just for beginners — it's how we catch the small technical drift that happens to every lifter over time, before it becomes a bigger problem. Film key working sets (not just warm-ups) from an angle that shows the full movement, and submit consistently as part of your regular check-in.

If a choice ever has to be made between hitting a heavier number and maintaining clean execution, the answer is always the same: drop the weight, keep the form. The load comes back. Bad movement patterns, repeated under load often enough, don't undo themselves.

02

Why Training Over 40 Is Different

The fundamentals of muscle building do not change after 40. Progressive overload, adequate protein, sufficient sleep — these principles are as effective at 45 as they were at 25. What does change is the recovery equation.

Testosterone and growth hormone decline progressively from the mid-30s. These hormones are the primary drivers of muscle protein synthesis and recovery speed. A 40-year-old man with optimised nutrition, sleep, and stress management will outperform a 40-year-old man who is under-eating and over-stressing — but neither will recover as quickly as they did at 22, and training programmes need to account for this.

The second major shift is in connective tissue. Tendons and ligaments have a lower collagen turnover rate than muscle tissue and are slower to adapt to increasing loads. This is why training-related injuries in men over 40 are overwhelmingly connective tissue injuries — not muscle tears. Load progression needs to be deliberate, not aggressive — which is exactly why form comes before load, every time.

The Reboot Principle

The goal is not to train like you are 25. The goal is to train like a well-coached 40-year-old — which, when done correctly, produces better long-term results than the undirected high-volume approach most men bring from their 20s.

03

Working Sets, Warm-Ups & Cooldowns

Every programmed set is a working set. We don't use a pyramid structure where you build up across several ascending sets to one top set. If three sets of an exercise are programmed, all three are working sets at the prescribed load and rep range — not a ramp-up.

Warm-ups happen before working sets begin, not woven into them. Each session — lower body, upper body, or full body/functional — has a specific warm-up sequence. Cooldowns follow every session, regardless of type.

General Rule, Every Session

10 minutes of easy cardio to raise core temperature, then before your first main lift: 50% of working weight for 8-12 reps, then 80-85% of working weight for 3-5 reps, then rest 2 minutes before starting Set 1 at full working weight. Use the full warm-up once per muscle group or movement pattern per session — if that body part comes up again later in the same workout, a light "feeler" set is enough to check setup, movement, and weight, not a full re-ramp. Warm up to prepare, not to fatigue.

🔹 Lower Body Strength Day — Warm-Up

  • 1Dynamic Mobility (2 min): Leg swings front/back + side/side, 10 each leg · World's Greatest Stretch, 5 reps per side · Bodyweight Cossack squats, 5 each side.
  • 2Muscle Activation (2 min): Glute bridges, 15 reps · Banded lateral walks, 10 steps each direction · Single-leg RDL (bodyweight), 5 each leg.
  • 3CNS Prep (2-3 min): Goblet squats, 2 sets of 5 reps (gradually increase weight) · Jump squats or broad jumps, 2 sets of 3 (explosive).

🔹 Upper Body Strength Day — Warm-Up

  • 1Mobility & Prep (2 min): Arm circles, 10 forward/back · Band dislocates or PVC pass-throughs, 10 reps · Cat-cow stretch, 30 seconds.
  • 2Activation (2 min): Banded pull-aparts, 15 reps · Wall angels, 10 reps · Scap push-ups, 10 reps.
  • 3Ramp-Up Sets (2-3 min): Empty bar or light dumbbell presses, 2 sets of 5-10 reps · gradual build-up to working sets.

🔹 Full Body / Functional Day — Warm-Up

  • 1Full-Body Movement Flow (3 min): Inchworms to cobra stretch, 5 reps · Lunge + twist, 6 reps per leg · Squat to stand, 5 reps.
  • 2Activation + Power (2 min): Banded rows, 12 reps · Box jumps or skater hops, 2 sets of 4 reps.
  • 3Heart Rate Up (1-2 min): Assault bike, row, or fast air squats, 60-90 seconds.

🧊 Post-Workout Cooldown — All Sessions

  • 1Breathing Downregulation (2 min): Lay on your back, feet up on a bench or wall. 4-second inhale, 6-second exhale through the nose. Focus on slowing your heart rate.
  • 2Targeted Stretching (2-3 min): Pigeon pose or seated glute stretch, 30 seconds each side · Chest opener (doorway stretch), 30 seconds each side · Hamstring stretch (floor or standing), 30 seconds per leg.

Why This Matters

Warming up properly improves joint mobility, muscle activation, mind-muscle connection, and performance and safety. Cooldowns support faster recovery, improved flexibility, cortisol regulation, and better sleep and readiness for your next session.

04

Double Progression — How Load Increases

Double progression is the method used to drive progressive overload in the Reboot programme. It removes the guesswork from "should I go heavier today" by giving you a clear, two-step rule to follow.

  • 1Step one — progress reps. Each exercise is programmed with a rep range (e.g. 8-12). Stay at the same weight and aim to add reps to your sets, session to session, until you can hit the top of the range.
  • 2Step two — progress load. Once you hit the top of the rep range on a given set, that's your signal to increase the weight on that set next session. Drop back toward the bottom of the rep range at the new load on that set, and the cycle repeats.

Each Set Progresses on Its Own

Progression is tracked per set, not across the exercise as a whole. If you hit the top of the rep range on Set 1 but not on Set 2 or Set 3, only Set 1's weight goes up next session — Sets 2 and 3 stay where they are until they individually earn the increase. This matters because fatigue accumulates across a session: your first set of an exercise is rarely under the same conditions as your third. Treating every set as its own independent progression means your loading stays accurate to what each set can actually handle, rather than being forced up or held back by what happened on a different set entirely.

Loads Should Allow You to Fail Within the Rep Range

Your working weight is chosen correctly when it allows you to genuinely fail within the programmed rep range — not before it, and not comfortably past the top of it. If you're hitting the top of your rep range with reps to spare, the load is too light: it's time to go heavier next session, provided form stays intact. If you can't get near the bottom of the range, the load is too heavy.

This is why submitted training footage matters here too — double progression only works if the reps you're logging are reps performed with full range of motion and clean technique, not partial reps that look good on paper but don't reflect real progress.

05

What Real Failure Actually Looks Like

Most men stop a set when it starts to feel hard — not when they've actually reached failure. There's a significant gap between "this is uncomfortable" and "I physically cannot complete another rep with good form," and that gap is where a huge amount of untapped progress is sitting unused.

Real failure is the point where, despite maximum effort, the rep does not complete — or only completes with a breakdown in form significant enough that it's no longer the same movement. Your bar speed slows dramatically on the concentric portion. You might grind, shake, or need to fight for the last few degrees of the movement. This is different from simply feeling fatigued, breathing hard, or experiencing the burn of muscular discomfort — all of which show up well before true failure and are not, on their own, a reason to rack the weight.

The Pattern We See Most

A set starts to burn or feel heavy around rep 7 of a 12-rep target, and the instinct is to stop at rep 8 or 9 because it "feels like enough." In reality, with genuine effort, most lifters have 2-4 more reps available even when a set feels maxed out — that gap between perceived effort and actual capacity is exactly what separates steady, unremarkable progress from genuinely significant results over time.

  • →Discomfort is not failure. The burning sensation in a muscle under load is a normal part of working close to failure — it shows up before true failure does, not at the same moment.
  • →Slowing down is not failure. A rep that's grinding but still moving, even slowly, is not a failed rep. Failure is when the bar stops moving entirely despite full effort, or when form breaks down enough that it's no longer a safe or valid repetition.
  • →This is exactly why form comes first. Pushing toward genuine failure only works — and stays safe — when your technique is sound. This is another reason submitted training footage matters: it lets your coach confirm you're working close to true failure with form that's still holding up, not just grinding out reps that look like progress but aren't safely executed.
06

The Training Structure

The Reboot training programme is built for hypertrophy — maximising lean muscle mass while systematically reducing body fat through the metabolic changes driven by the Ignition Protocol.

  • 📅Frequency: 3-5 sessions per week, not more. Sessions run 45-75 minutes. Consistent session timing supports circadian hormone rhythms — morning training aligns well with the natural cortisol peak.
  • 🚫Don't add more. Your results come from the maximum stimulus you can actually recover from — not from maximum volume. Adding extra sessions or sets beyond what's programmed doesn't accelerate results; it just outpaces your recovery capacity and works against the programme.
  • 📊Progressive Overload: Driven by double progression (see above) — reps first, then load, always within the bounds of clean technique.
  • 🔁Deload Weeks: Triggered by a genuine dip in performance, energy, or recovery markers — not by a fixed every-4-to-6-weeks calendar. If your numbers are climbing and you feel strong, keep going. If performance stalls or drops, energy is consistently low, or your waking temp/RHR trend is heading the wrong way, that's the signal to deload — volume and intensity reduced by approximately 40-50% for the week.
07

Cardio: Rarely Programmed, Always Purposeful

Cardio is rarely a programmed part of the Reboot training structure. When it is included, it's minimal and falls into one of two categories — and the two are not interchangeable, because they serve completely different purposes.

🚶
LISS (Default)
Low-intensity steady state — walking, easy cycling, swimming. The default when cardio is included at all. Minimal cortisol cost, doesn't compete with recovery from your hypertrophy sessions.
⚡
HIIT (Selective)
Programmed in some plans in small amounts, specifically for cardiovascular health, HRV improvement, and longevity — not as a fat-burning tool. If it's in your programme, it's there for a specific physiological reason your coach has identified.

A Common Misconception

If HIIT appears in your programme, it is not there to "burn more fat" — fat loss in this system comes from the Ignition Protocol and your training stimulus, not from chasing a calorie burn through cardio. Adding your own extra HIIT or cardio sessions on top of the programme works against recovery and is exactly the kind of "more isn't better" addition covered above.

08

The Recovery Hierarchy

Most men focus 90% of their attention on the training session and almost none on recovery. This is the wrong distribution. Training is the stimulus. Recovery is where the adaptation happens. If recovery is compromised, the training session is largely wasted.

The Reboot recovery hierarchy — in order of impact:

  • 1Sleep: 7.5-9 hours of quality sleep is the single most powerful anabolic intervention available. Growth hormone is predominantly secreted during deep sleep. Testosterone production is tightly linked to sleep duration and quality. A chronically sleep-restricted man will not build muscle effectively regardless of everything else.
  • 2Nutrition: Post-training protein and carbohydrates within 60 minutes of finishing a session. Muscle protein synthesis remains elevated for 24-48 hours post-training — overall daily protein intake matters more than any single meal window, but the post-train window is the highest-leverage opportunity to support it.
  • 3Stress load: Psychological stress and training stress draw from the same cortisol budget. A high-stress work period is a legitimate reason to reduce training volume — not a reason to train harder to "release tension." Managing your total stress load is a performance variable.
  • 4Intra-workout nutrition: Fast-digesting carbohydrates during training (e.g. Gatorade, fruit, rice cakes) blunt cortisol, preserve muscle glycogen, and meaningfully improve training performance and recovery — particularly for sessions over 60 minutes.
  • 5Supplementation: Magnesium glycinate pre-sleep, creatine monohydrate daily, collagen peptides post-training (with vitamin C). These are the evidence-backed basics — everything else is secondary.
09

Cortisol: The Hidden Variable

Cortisol is the body's primary stress hormone — produced by the adrenal glands in response to any stressor, whether physical (a training session), psychological (work pressure), or physiological (inadequate sleep, under-eating). It is not inherently bad. The natural morning cortisol peak is what gets you out of bed and into the gym. The problem is chronically elevated cortisol.

Chronic cortisol elevation drives: muscle breakdown (catabolism), fat storage particularly around the midsection, suppressed testosterone production, impaired sleep quality, and blunted immune function. It is the single biggest recovery saboteur for men over 40 — and it is almost entirely driven by lifestyle variables, not training volume alone.

Cortisol Red Flags

Waking between 2-4am, carrying fat preferentially around the belly despite training, low libido, slow recovery between sessions, persistent low-level anxiety or irritability, poor stress tolerance. If this is your picture — the recovery protocol matters as much as the training programme.

The Reboot stress management system and HRV resources address this directly. Recovery is a skill — and it is one that pays compound interest over a training career.

10

Tracking Progress the Right Way

Progress tracking in the Reboot Method is multi-dimensional. Scale weight is one data point among many — and one of the least reliable for men doing a simultaneous muscle gain and fat loss phase (body recomposition).

🎥
Training Footage
Submitted regularly for coach review. The earliest and most direct signal of whether your technique is holding up as load increases.
💪
Training Logs
Are you progressing through double progression — reps first, then load? Consistent progression is the clearest signal the programme is working.
📸
Progress Photos
Same lighting, same time of day, every 4 weeks. Visual change tells a story the scale cannot. Particularly useful in recomp phases.
🌡
Waking Temp
Rising waking temperature confirms metabolic recovery is progressing — the most direct confirmation the nutrition protocol is working.

DEXA scans every 3-6 months remain the gold standard for an objective body composition readout — corrected lean mass and fat mass percentages, tracked over time.
