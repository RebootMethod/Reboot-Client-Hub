Training Principles

Training & Recovery
Over 40

Training in your 40s is not the same game as training in your 20s — and the men who try to run the same playbook eventually pay for it. The Reboot Method is not about training less. It is about training smarter, recovering harder, and building a body that gets stronger every year rather than grinding itself down.

Key Takeaways

  • Recovery capacity — not training volume — is the limiting factor for men over 40.
  • Hypertrophy training (5 days per week, progressive overload) remains highly effective and is the primary training mode.
  • Sleep is your most powerful anabolic tool. No supplement comes close.
  • Cortisol management is as important as the training session itself.
  • Double-progression (reps first, then load) is the tracking method used in the Reboot coaching app.
01

Why Training Over 40 Is Different

The fundamentals of muscle building do not change after 40. Progressive overload, adequate protein, sufficient sleep — these principles are as effective at 45 as they were at 25. What does change is the recovery equation.

Testosterone and growth hormone decline progressively from the mid-30s. These hormones are the primary drivers of muscle protein synthesis and recovery speed. A 40-year-old man with optimised nutrition, sleep, and stress management will outperform a 40-year-old man who is under-eating and over-stressing — but neither will recover as quickly as they did at 22, and training programmes need to account for this.

The second major shift is in connective tissue. Tendons and ligaments have a lower collagen turnover rate than muscle tissue and are slower to adapt to increasing loads. This is why training-related injuries in men over 40 are overwhelmingly connective tissue injuries — not muscle tears. Load progression needs to be deliberate, not aggressive.

The Reboot Principle

The goal is not to train like you are 25. The goal is to train like a well-coached 40-year-old — which, when done correctly, produces better long-term results than the undirected high-volume approach most men bring from their 20s.

02

The Training Structure

The Reboot training programme is built for hypertrophy — maximising lean muscle mass while systematically reducing body fat through the metabolic changes driven by the Ignition Protocol. The structure is five sessions per week, typically programmed as a push/pull/legs or upper/lower split depending on individual schedule and recovery capacity.

  • 📅Frequency: 5 sessions per week. Sessions run 45–75 minutes. Consistent session timing (the Reboot default is 7:30am) supports circadian hormone rhythms — morning training aligns well with the natural cortisol peak.
  • 📊Progressive Overload: The non-negotiable. If you are not progressively increasing either reps or load session to session, you are maintaining at best. Progress in the Reboot app is tracked via double-progression — hit the top of your rep range across all sets, then increase weight next session.
  • 🔁Deload Weeks: Programmed every 4–6 weeks. Volume and intensity are reduced by approximately 40–50%. This is not optional recovery — it is where the adaptation actually consolidates. Skipping deloads is one of the most common reasons progress stalls.
  • ⚡Cardio: Low-intensity steady state (LISS) — walking, cycling, swimming — is preferred. High-intensity cardio on top of a 5-day hypertrophy programme significantly increases the cortisol load and competes with recovery. If you want to do more, walk more.
03

The Recovery Hierarchy

Most men focus 90% of their attention on the training session and almost none on recovery. This is the wrong distribution. Training is the stimulus. Recovery is where the adaptation happens. If recovery is compromised, the training session is largely wasted.

The Reboot recovery hierarchy — in order of impact:

  • 1Sleep: 7.5–9 hours of quality sleep is the single most powerful anabolic intervention available. Growth hormone is predominantly secreted during deep sleep. Testosterone production is tightly linked to sleep duration and quality. A chronically sleep-restricted man will not build muscle effectively regardless of everything else.
  • 2Nutrition: Post-training protein and carbohydrates within 60 minutes of finishing a session. Muscle protein synthesis remains elevated for 24–48 hours post-training — overall daily protein intake matters more than any single meal window, but the post-train window is the highest-leverage opportunity to support it.
  • 3Stress load: Psychological stress and training stress draw from the same cortisol budget. A high-stress work period is a legitimate reason to reduce training volume — not a reason to train harder to "release tension." Managing your total stress load is a performance variable.
  • 4Intra-workout nutrition: Fast-digesting carbohydrates during training (e.g. Gatorade, fruit, rice cakes) blunt cortisol, preserve muscle glycogen, and meaningfully improve training performance and recovery — particularly for sessions over 60 minutes.
  • 5Supplementation: Magnesium glycinate pre-sleep, creatine monohydrate daily, collagen peptides post-training (with vitamin C). These are the evidence-backed basics — everything else is secondary.
04

Cortisol: The Hidden Variable

Cortisol is the body's primary stress hormone — produced by the adrenal glands in response to any stressor, whether physical (a training session), psychological (work pressure), or physiological (inadequate sleep, under-eating). It is not inherently bad. The natural morning cortisol peak is what gets you out of bed and into the gym. The problem is chronically elevated cortisol.

Chronic cortisol elevation drives: muscle breakdown (catabolism), fat storage particularly around the midsection, suppressed testosterone production, impaired sleep quality, and blunted immune function. It is the single biggest recovery saboteur for men over 40 — and it is almost entirely driven by lifestyle variables, not training volume alone.

Cortisol Red Flags

Waking between 2–4am, carrying fat preferentially around the belly despite training, low libido, slow recovery between sessions, persistent low-level anxiety or irritability, poor stress tolerance. If this is your picture — the recovery protocol matters as much as the training programme.

The Reboot stress management system and HRV resources address this directly. Recovery is a skill — and it is one that pays compound interest over a training career.

05

Tracking Progress the Right Way

Progress tracking in the Reboot Method is multi-dimensional. Scale weight is one data point among many — and one of the least reliable for men doing a simultaneous muscle gain and fat loss phase (body recomposition).

💪
Training Logs
Are you lifting more, or doing more reps at the same load? Consistent progressive overload is the clearest signal the programme is working.
📸
Progress Photos
Same lighting, same time of day, every 4 weeks. Visual change tells a story the scale cannot. Particularly useful in recomp phases.
🌡
Waking Temp
Rising waking temperature confirms metabolic recovery is progressing — the most direct confirmation the nutrition protocol is working.
📊
DEXA Scans
Every 3–6 months for an objective body composition readout. Corrected lean mass and fat mass percentages. The gold standard.
