Recovery & Lifestyle

Shift Work &
Non-Standard Hours

Most sleep and lifestyle advice assumes a normal 9-to-5 day with sunrise on your way to work. If you're on an early start, a night shift, or a rotating roster, that advice doesn't translate cleanly — and applying it word-for-word can actually work against you. Here's how the same principles adapt to your actual schedule.

Key Takeaways

  • The underlying science doesn't change — light, meal timing, and sleep consistency still drive everything. What changes is when you apply each lever.
  • If you can't get natural morning light because it's dark, a light therapy device is the most reliable substitute.
  • Night shift workers should treat their "wake time" as whenever their day begins — even if that's 9pm.
  • Rotating rosters are the hardest pattern to manage. The goal shifts from optimisation to damage control during transition periods.
  • Your coach needs your actual schedule to set realistic targets — generic advice will frustrate you more than help you.
01

The Principle Doesn't Change, the Timing Does

Everything in the Sleep: The Reboot Protocols guide and the morning light section of Healthy Lifestyle Habits is built around circadian biology — light exposure sets your internal clock, that clock governs cortisol and melatonin timing, and those hormones govern sleep quality and energy. None of that changes if you work non-standard hours.

What changes is when those levers apply. "Get morning light within 30 minutes of waking" assumes waking happens at sunrise. If you're up at 4:30am in winter, or your "morning" is actually 9pm before a night shift, the instruction is the same — light exposure shortly after you wake, before your main sleep period — but the clock time looks completely different.

The Reframe

Stop thinking about "morning light" and "evening wind-down" as fixed clock times. Think about them as "light shortly after your wake-up" and "dim, low-stimulation wind-down before your main sleep period" — wherever those fall on the clock for you.

02

Scenario: Early Starts With No Morning Light

Pre-Dawn Wake, Standard Daytime Schedule

This is the most common non-standard pattern — up well before sunrise, with two or more hours of darkness before natural light becomes available. The risk here isn't your sleep timing itself (you're still working a roughly normal day), it's that the morning light signal that's supposed to anchor your circadian rhythm simply isn't available when you need it.

  • 💡Use a light therapy device. A 10,000 lux light box, used for 15-20 minutes shortly after waking, is the most reliable substitute for natural morning sunlight. This is well-supported in shift work and seasonal light research — it's not a lesser option, it's a legitimate tool for exactly this situation.
  • 🔆Maximise indoor light regardless. Turn on all the lights in your home immediately on waking, even before the light box. Bright indoor light is still meaningfully better than dim light, even if it's not as strong a signal as sunlight or a dedicated light box.
  • ☀️Get outside the moment it's light enough, even if that's an hour or two after you've started your day. A second, smaller light exposure once the sun is up still reinforces the signal — it doesn't have to be all at once.
  • 🌙Protect the other end of the day. Since you're compensating for a weaker morning signal, the evening wind-down (dim lights, no screens, consistent bedtime) becomes even more important to reinforce the circadian boundary from the other direction.
03

Scenario: Night Shift

Working Overnight, Sleeping During the Day

Night shift inverts the entire framework — and the goal is to actually commit to the inversion, not try to live in both worlds at once. The men who struggle most on night shift are usually the ones trying to partially maintain a daytime schedule on their days off, which keeps their body clock permanently confused.

  • 🌅Treat your shift start as "morning." Get bright light exposure (light box, or just bright indoor lighting) at the start of your shift, the same way a day-shift worker would use morning sunlight. This signals "day has started" to your circadian system, just on an inverted clock.
  • 🕶Wear sunglasses on the drive home. Morning sunlight on your commute home after a night shift is one of the biggest disruptors to falling asleep afterward — it tells your brain the day is starting right when you need to wind down. Dark sunglasses on the way home meaningfully blunt this signal.
  • 🛏Make your sleep environment as dark as physically possible. Blackout curtains are non-negotiable for daytime sleep — even small amounts of daylight leaking in suppress melatonin far more than equivalent light would at night.
  • 🥛Run the pre-sleep protocol before your daytime sleep, exactly as described in the Sleep guide — same warm milk, honey, and sea salt approach, just timed before your main sleep block rather than at a conventional bedtime.
  • 📅Try to keep days off consistent with your shift pattern where possible, rather than flipping back to a normal daytime schedule for 1-2 days then back to nights. The constant flipping is harder on your system than staying inverted throughout your work stretch.
04

Scenario: Rotating Rosters

No Fixed Schedule, Pattern Changes Week to Week

This is genuinely the hardest pattern to manage, and it's worth being honest about that upfront. A fixed night shift, however inverted, is at least consistent — your body can adapt to one stable pattern. A rotating roster never lets that adaptation fully happen, because the pattern keeps shifting before your circadian system catches up.

The goal on a rotating roster shifts from optimisation to damage control during transitions, with a return to the full protocol whenever you land on a stable stretch. Trying to apply a rigid, optimised routine across constantly changing shifts will mostly just create frustration when it doesn't work — the more useful mindset is triage.

  • →Prioritise light exposure timed to whichever shift you're currently on — bright light at your current "start of day," dim and dark before your current "end of day" — and accept that this target moves every time your roster changes.
  • →Protect sleep duration over sleep timing during transition periods. When the clock keeps moving, getting 7+ hours matters more than getting it at a "correct" time — some sleep debt accumulated during a rough transition is recoverable; chronic sleep restriction is not.
  • →Use the Classic Sleep Protocol every single sleep period, regardless of what time of day it falls on. This is one lever that doesn't need to change with your schedule — it works the same whether your "bedtime" is 10pm or 10am.
  • →Expect the transition days to feel rough — and don't let that derail nutrition and training consistency on top of disrupted sleep. One system breaking down (sleep) is hard enough to manage without letting it cascade into your eating and training falling apart too.

An Honest Note

If your roster genuinely never stabilises, talk to your coach about adjusting training and check-in expectations around your actual pattern — including more flexible deload triggers, since fatigue from roster transitions can look identical to training fatigue on paper. We'd rather build a programme that fits your real schedule than one that assumes a consistency you don't have.

05

Nutrition Timing on Non-Standard Hours

The Ignition Protocol's meal timing principles — carbs around training, protein and carbs in the post-workout window, the pre-sleep protocol before your main sleep period — all still apply. What shifts is when "pre-sleep" and "post-workout" actually fall on the clock, which depends entirely on when you're training and sleeping, not on what time it says on a wall clock.

  • →Train when you have the most energy and fewest interruptions, which for shift workers is often not first thing after waking. Some night shift workers train before their shift starts (their "afternoon" equivalent); others train right after waking from daytime sleep. Both can work — consistency matters more than which one you pick.
  • →Don't skip the post-workout window just because the clock looks unusual. If you finish training at 11pm before a night shift, that post-workout protein and carb window still matters exactly as much as it would at 11am.
  • →Caffeine cutoff is relative to your sleep period, not the clock. If your main sleep block starts at 9am, your caffeine cutoff is roughly 6 hours before that — even if that means no caffeine after 3am during a night shift.
