Supplements:
What Actually Works

The supplement industry is worth billions because it sells hope in capsule form. Most of it is marketing. A small number of compounds have genuine, replicated evidence behind them. This guide tells you exactly what to take, what dose, and what to skip.

Key Takeaways

  • Five supplements have strong evidence for active men over 40: creatine, magnesium, vitamin D, collagen peptides, and zinc.
  • Supplements build on top of a solid nutrition foundation — they do not replace one.
  • Most testosterone boosters, fat burners, and pre-workouts are either ineffective, overhyped, or reliant on stimulants.
  • Timing matters for some compounds (creatine, collagen) but is largely irrelevant for others (vitamin D, zinc).
  • Deficiency correction (vitamin D, magnesium, zinc) has a higher ROI than performance optimisation for most men.

Tier 1 — Take These Daily

These are the compounds with the strongest evidence base, lowest risk profile, and highest impact for active men over 40. If your supplement stack does not include these, start here before anything else.

Creatine Monohydrate
5g daily — any time
The most researched performance supplement in existence. Increases phosphocreatine stores in muscle, directly improving output in high-intensity training (more reps, more weight, faster recovery between sets). Also has meaningful cognitive benefits — improved memory and processing speed — that are particularly relevant for men over 40. Monohydrate is the form. No loading phase needed. Timing is irrelevant. Take it
Magnesium Glycinate
300–400mg before bed
Magnesium is a cofactor in over 300 enzymatic reactions — including ATP production, muscle contraction, protein synthesis, and sleep regulation. Deficiency is extremely common in active men (training depletes magnesium through sweat). Glycinate is the preferred form: high bioavailability, gentle on the gut, and has a mild relaxing effect that supports sleep onset. Avoid magnesium oxide — it is cheap but poorly absorbed. Take it
Vitamin D3 + K2
3,000–5,000 IU D3 + 100mcg K2 daily
Vitamin D functions as a hormone, not just a vitamin. It has receptors in virtually every tissue including the testes — where it directly supports testosterone synthesis. Deficiency (below 40 ng/mL) is associated with suppressed testosterone, poor immune function, impaired muscle recovery, and low mood. K2 directs calcium to bones rather than arteries — always pair these. Take with a fat-containing meal for best absorption. Take it

Tier 2 — Targeted & Contextual

These compounds have solid evidence behind them but are more contextual — most relevant for specific training goals, deficiencies, or recovery requirements.

Collagen Peptides
15–20g post-training with vitamin C
Collagen is the primary structural protein in tendons, ligaments, cartilage, and skin. Supplementing with hydrolysed collagen peptides alongside vitamin C (which drives collagen synthesis) in the post-training window has been shown to improve connective tissue repair and reduce injury risk — particularly relevant for men over 40 where connective tissue adaptation lags behind muscle adaptation. Does not replace whey or other complete protein for muscle protein synthesis. Take it
Zinc
15–30mg with dinner
Zinc is essential for testosterone synthesis, immune function, wound healing, and protein metabolism. Deficiency is common in men with high training volume or poor dietary variety. Take with food to reduce nausea. Do not exceed 40mg daily — chronic high-dose zinc supplementation depletes copper. If using a zinc-magnesium formula (ZMA), confirm the dosages are appropriate before adding additional zinc. If deficient
Omega-3 Fish Oil
2–3g EPA+DHA daily with food
EPA and DHA reduce systemic inflammation, support cardiovascular health, and improve muscle protein synthesis when combined with adequate protein. Most relevant if your dietary omega-3 intake is low (i.e. you are not eating oily fish 3x per week). The anti-inflammatory effect is particularly useful for men training through chronic stress or with elevated C-reactive protein on bloodwork. If low dietary intake

Tier 3 — Skip These

These are the supplements most commonly marketed to men over 40 that either have weak evidence, rely on stimulants, or are outright misleading in their claims.

Testosterone Boosters
—
The category is built almost entirely on marketing. Most "testosterone boosters" contain underdosed ashwagandha, fenugreek, or tribulus terrestris at levels far below what research uses. The few compounds with legitimate evidence (ashwagandha at 600mg KSM-66, for example) are available individually for a fraction of the price. If your testosterone is genuinely low, fix your sleep, nutrition, and stress first — then test again. Skip it
Fat Burners
—
Primarily stimulant blends (caffeine, synephrine, yohimbine) with small thermogenic effects that are negligible against a well-structured nutrition plan. Any meaningful effect is from the caffeine — which you can get from coffee for pennies. The metabolic impact on fat loss over a 12-week programme is negligible. The side effects (elevated heart rate, disrupted sleep, adrenal stress) are not. Skip it
BCAAs
—
Branched-chain amino acids are redundant if you are hitting your daily protein target from whole food sources. Your protein foods already contain leucine, isoleucine, and valine in the proportions needed to trigger muscle protein synthesis. The only context where BCAAs have marginal benefit is training in a fully fasted state — which is not the Reboot protocol. Save your money for food. Skip it

PW
