Sleep:
The Reboot Protocols

Sleep is not rest. It's the highest-leverage performance intervention available to you — bigger than any supplement, any training tweak, any macro adjustment. This page covers why it matters and exactly what to do about it, including the non-negotiable nightly protocol every Reboot client runs.

Key Takeaways

  • The 2-4am wake-up is a blood sugar and cortisol problem, not a "bad sleeper" problem — and it has a direct fix.
  • The Classic Sleep Protocol (warm milk, honey, sea salt) is non-negotiable every single night in the Reboot system.
  • A fixed daily wake time is the single most powerful sleep intervention — more impactful than anything you do at night.
  • Deep sleep and REM sleep respond to different levers. Know which one you're actually targeting.
  • Alcohol is the most underestimated sleep disruptor — its damage peaks during the exact hours you need REM sleep most.
01

Why Sleep Is the Highest-Leverage Lever You Have

Every part of the Reboot system — the Ignition Protocol, your training programme, your supplement stack — works on top of a sleep foundation. Get sleep wrong and everything else underperforms. Get it right and everything else works better than it should.

During deep sleep, growth hormone release peaks, muscle tissue repairs, and the liver and immune system carry out maintenance work that doesn't happen efficiently while you're awake. During REM sleep, the brain consolidates memory, processes emotional load, and restores cognitive sharpness. Testosterone production is also tightly coupled to total sleep time — most of your daily testosterone output happens during sleep, not while you're awake.

The Performance Framing

Don't think of sleep as "rest" or "wellness." Think of it as a performance variable with the same status as your training programme or your macro targets — because mechanistically, that's exactly what it is.

02

The 2-4am Wake-Up: What's Actually Happening

This is one of the most common sleep complaints from Reboot clients, and it is rarely random. Waking consistently between 2-4am is most often driven by a blood glucose drop that triggers a cortisol and adrenaline response — your body's emergency mechanism for raising blood sugar back up, which also wakes you up in the process.

This pattern is especially common in men who are under-eating carbohydrate, training hard, or going long stretches without food before bed. The fix is almost always nutritional before it's anything else.

  • 1Stabilise blood sugar overnight with the pre-bed protocol below — this is the single most direct fix for the 2-4am wake-up pattern.
  • 2Reduce evening cortisol load. Avoid intense training or bright light exposure late in the evening — both keep cortisol elevated when it should be declining.
  • 3Magnesium glycinate before bed supports nervous system downregulation and has decades of clinical evidence behind it for sleep quality.
  • 4Watch caffeine timing. Caffeine has a long half-life — a 2pm coffee can still be meaningfully active in your system at 9pm.
  • 5Slow nasal breathing if you do wake — a few minutes of slow, deliberate nasal breathing downregulates the sympathetic nervous system and helps you fall back asleep rather than lying awake wired.

When to Escalate

If the 2-4am pattern persists despite consistent nutrition and lifestyle intervention, it's worth ruling out sleep apnoea or other physical causes with a GP. Not every sleep issue is a coaching fix — know when to redirect to a doctor.

The Classic Sleep Protocol — Non-Negotiable

Every Reboot client runs this every single night, no exceptions. It directly addresses the mechanism behind the 2-4am wake-up before it has a chance to start.

🥛 The Classic Sleep Protocol
Warm whole milk 250ml · Raw honey 15-20g · Sea salt 1 pinch

Warm the milk gently (don't boil). Stir in honey and salt. Drink 30 minutes before bed. Every night. No exceptions.

Why it works: The liver depletes its glycogen stores overnight. When it runs out — typically 2-4am — the body releases cortisol to raise blood sugar, which disrupts sleep architecture and can catabolise muscle tissue in the process. Honey loads liver glycogen before bed, preventing the shortage that triggers the cortisol release. Sea salt supports overnight adrenal function. The casein protein in milk provides a slow, steady release of amino acids through the night.

Once the classic protocol is a locked-in habit (usually 1-2 weeks), there's one upgrade worth adding:

🥛 Honey Glycine Milk — The Upgrade
Warm whole milk 250ml · Raw honey 15g · Collagen peptides 5g · Sea salt 1 pinch

Same method as the classic protocol — warm milk, dissolve in the collagen, honey, and salt.

Why it works: Collagen is rich in glycine, an amino acid that lowers core body temperature at sleep onset — the actual physiological trigger for entering deep sleep. Faster deep sleep onset means more total time in the growth-hormone-dominant sleep stages. If you make one addition to the classic protocol, make it this one — the improvement in sleep depth is typically noticeable within a week.

For clients who are lactose intolerant or simply don't like milk, the mechanism still works without dairy:

🍲 Honey Bone Broth (Dairy-Free Version)
Beef bone broth 300ml · Raw honey 15g · Sea salt 1 pinch

Heat the broth, stir in honey and salt, drink warm 30 minutes before bed.

Why it works: Bone broth provides glycine and minerals — the same sleep-onset mechanism as the collagen upgrade above. Honey loads liver glycogen exactly as in the milk version. This is not a compromise version — for clients with gut sensitivity to dairy, the bone broth version may actually produce better sleep outcomes.

04

The Foundational Habit: Fixed Wake Time

If a client has erratic, unpredictable sleep with no clear cause, the single highest-impact intervention — bigger than any supplement or pre-sleep protocol — is a fixed daily wake time, seven days a week, including weekends.

Your circadian rhythm is a biological clock that governs cortisol, melatonin, and core body temperature across the 24-hour cycle. An inconsistent wake time is one of the most underrated sleep disruptors — it sends conflicting signals to that clock every single day, which fragments sleep architecture even when total sleep duration looks adequate on paper.

  • →Pick one wake time and hold it — even on weekends, even after a bad night. This is the anchor everything else is built around.
  • →Get morning light within 30 minutes of waking — direct sunlight if possible, even on an overcast day. This reinforces the circadian signal your fixed wake time is already sending.
  • →Cut caffeine by 1pm. Caffeine's half-life means an afternoon coffee is still partially active at bedtime, blunting sleep pressure when you need it most.
  • →No bright light after 9pm. Bright overhead lighting and screens suppress melatonin release at exactly the time your body should be ramping it up.

Coach Note

If a client says they already do "all the good sleep habits" and it's still not working, the root cause may be physical rather than behavioural — refer to a GP rather than continuing to stack lifestyle interventions on top of each other.

Deep Sleep vs. REM Sleep — Different Levers

Sleep quality isn't one variable — deep (slow-wave) sleep and REM sleep are distinct stages, driven by different mechanisms, and respond to different interventions. Knowing which one you're targeting changes what you should actually do.

Training timingDeep sleepIntense training too close to bedtime keeps core temperature and cortisol elevated, delaying deep sleep onset.
Consistent wake timeBothAnchors circadian rhythm, which governs the timing and proportion of every sleep stage across the night.
Room temperatureDeep sleepCore body temperature needs to drop to initiate and sustain deep sleep. A cool room (17-20°C) supports this directly.
Magnesium glycinateDeep sleepMagnesium deficiency directly suppresses slow-wave sleep. Glycinate form is most bioavailable and non-laxative.
AlcoholREM sleep (severely)Alcohol's disruptive effect concentrates in the second half of the night — exactly when REM sleep should dominate.
Caffeine cutoffDeep sleepCaffeine's long half-life means afternoon intake is still active at bedtime, delaying sleep onset and reducing deep sleep time.
Evening stress/cortisolREM sleepElevated evening cortisol suppresses REM sleep proportionally — managing evening stress load protects this stage specifically.
Total sleep durationREM sleep (disproportionately)REM sleep is back-loaded toward the second half of the night. Cutting sleep short costs REM time disproportionately compared to deep sleep.
06

Alcohol: The Most Underestimated Disruptor

Even one to two drinks measurably suppresses overnight HRV and sleep quality — and the damage isn't evenly spread across the night. The disruptive effect peaks during the 2-4am window, while your body is metabolising the alcohol — exactly the hours when REM sleep and overnight recovery should be at their highest.

This means alcohol doesn't just cost you "a bit of sleep quality" in a general sense — it specifically targets the back half of the night where REM sleep is concentrated, and overlaps directly with the same hours the Classic Sleep Protocol is designed to protect. The two effects compound in opposite directions: skip the pre-sleep protocol and drink, and you've maximised the conditions for a 2-4am disruption.
